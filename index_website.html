üìöProject Overview

This comprehensive digital humanities project presents an interactive geographical and temporal analysis of Islamic books published in Chinese during the late Qing Dynasty and Republican period (1863-1943). Through advanced GIS visualization techniques, we map the spatial distribution, temporal evolution, and migration patterns of 247 books across 12 Chinese publishing cities to 10 Japanese institutional repositories.

The project integrates multiple data sources including bibliographic records, expert-curated metadata (58 fields), scholarly commentary (~12,000 characters), and OCR-extracted catalog data (16,809 characters from Tenri University), providing unprecedented insight into Sino-Japanese knowledge networks and the preservation of Chinese Islamic intellectual heritage.

3. Regional Intellectual Geography and Specialization

Beijing's specialization in newspapers and periodicals reflects the urban print culture analyzed in Rudolf G. Wagner's The Contemporary Chinese Historical Drama: Four Studies (1990). Conversely, Yunnan and Sichuan's concentration in theological manuscripts demonstrates provincial scholarly networks documented in Jonathan Lipman's Familiar Strangers: A History of Muslims in Northwest China (1997). These geographic specialization patterns are consistent with the broader regional intellectual divisions described in Susan Naquin and Evelyn S. Rawski's Chinese Society in the Eighteenth Century (1987), suggesting continuity from late imperial to Republican periods.

References: Wagner (1990), Lipman (1997), Naquin & Rawski (1987)
